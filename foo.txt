The student will describe an animal&rsquo;s niche and compare niches of different species.</span></div><div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;"><span>HOMEOSTASIS&nbsp;</span><br /><span>&#8203;An ecosystem seeks balance, and that balance is affected by the population of particular species or the introduction of a new species.</span></div><div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;"><span>&weierp; The student will describe how animal populations tend toward homeostasis.&nbsp;</span><br /><br /><span>&weierp; The student will describe the factors that determine the carrying capacity of a habitat and how it impacts animal population cycles.&nbsp;</span><br /><br /><span>&#8203;&weierp; The student will be able to explain an environmental flow.</span><br /><br /><span>&#8203;&weierp; The student will be able to give examples of animals that create environmental flows within a corridor.</span></div><div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;"><span>INTERACTIONS&nbsp;</span><br /><span>&#8203;An ecosystem is composed of complex interactions between animals, plants, water and soil.</span></div><div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;"><span>&#8203;&weierp; The student will identify interactions within an ecosystem.</span></div><div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;"><span>COMMUNITY&nbsp;</span><br /><span>&#8203;Animals and plants occupy specific areas and function through interrelated trophic systems.</span></div><div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;"><span>&weierp; The student will discuss how human behavior affects animal populations.</span></div><div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;"><span>BIODIVERSITY&nbsp;</span><br /><span>&#8203;An ecosystem requires a variety of species to remain stable.</span></div><div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;"><span>&weierp; The student will describe the role animals play as consumers in nutrient cycling and energy flow.&nbsp;</span><br /><br /><span>&weierp; The student will describe ways animals impact plants, soil and water quality in an environment.&nbsp;</span><br /><br /><span>&#8203;&weierp; The student will explain the flow of energy through the trophic levels of a terrestrial habitat (food pyramid).&nbsp;</span></div><div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;"><span>&#8203;RESILIENCE&nbsp;</span><br /><span>&#8203;Animals are able to survive a variety of environmental pressures.</span></div><div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;"><span>&weierp; The student will give examples of introduced species and discuss their ability to survive in various environments.&nbsp;</span><br /><br /><span>&#8203;&weierp; The student will identify causes of threatened, endangered and sensitive animal populations.</span></div><div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;"><span>CAREER&nbsp;</span><br /><span>&#8203;The study of animals offers a wide range of career opportunities.&#8203;</span></div><div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;"><span>&weierp; The student will complete tasks that wildlife biologists and population ecologists perform.</span></div><div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;"><strong><font size="4">&#8203;Animals Field Study Activities</font></strong><br />The following activities are typical of a day on Animals Field Study at Outdoor School. Actual activities will vary by site and season.<br /><br /><strong>Nutrient Cycle/Decomposition:</strong>&nbsp;Students examine decomposition and study the means by which nutrients are cycled through an ecosystem.<br /><br /><strong>Adaptations Activities:</strong>&nbsp;Students identify adaptations of animals and describe how those adaptations help animals survive in their habitats. They use measurement and observations to identify animals using reference materials such as field guides.<br />The most common activities:<br /><strong>Pelts<br />Skulls<br />Horns, Antlers and Hooves<br />Birds<br />Live Animal Stations</strong><br /><br /><strong>Arthropod study:</strong>&nbsp;Students find and examine arthropods and discuss the role arthropods play in local ecosystems.<br /><br /><strong>Food Web:</strong>&nbsp;Students demonstrate interrelationships among structures in an ecosystem.<br /><br /><strong>Animal Signs Hike and Survey/Casting Animal Tracks:</strong>&nbsp;Students identify a wildlife-related question for inquiry and then survey the site for signs of wildlife and draw conclusions about presence and populations of animals.<br /><br /><strong>Bear Game:</strong>&nbsp;Students use role-playing and simulation to explore concepts of carrying capacity and competition for food, water, shelter and space.<br /><br /><strong>Oh Deer!:</strong>&nbsp;Students role play deer population cycles to discuss carrying capacity and wildlife population cycles.<br /><br /><strong>Bird Observations:</strong>&nbsp;Students perform a bird population survey by counting and cataloging bird songs and observed birds.<br /><br /><strong>Food Pyramid:</strong>&nbsp;Students study the numerical relations in a terrestrial food pyramid.<br /><br /><strong>&#8203;Deadly Links:</strong>&nbsp;Students actively simulate the process of bioaccumulation of toxins in a wildlife population.
